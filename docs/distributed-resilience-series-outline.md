# Distributed Resilience: A Three-Part Series

**Series thesis:** Distributed renewable energy is security infrastructure. Two case studies prove it. One policy argument follows.

**Target publication:** Bloomberg Green / Bloomberg Opinion (series format). Fallback: Foreign Policy (single long piece).

**Ask Shank Monday (March 16):** Does he have Bloomberg contacts? Where does this land best?

---

## Part 1: Hard Security — Ukraine

**Thesis:** Russia's strategy of targeting centralized energy infrastructure to break Ukrainian society is failing in communities that went decentralized. Distributed renewables aren't climate policy — they're a counterstrategy.

**Structure:**
- Open with a specific community. A mayor describing what happens when the grid goes down for the 50th time — and what changed when they got solar.
- Russia's theory of victory: attrit civilian infrastructure → societal fatigue → political pressure to concede. Cite the systematic targeting of power plants, substations, grid infrastructure.
- The counter: when a school has its own solar array, that attack vector fails. You can't knock out what isn't connected to the grid.
- Ecoaction/Ecoclub/Greenpeace UA on the ground — what they're seeing, what communities are requesting, how fast decentralized installs are happening.
- Security expert on why this matters strategically, not just humanitarianly.
- Close with scale: what would it look like if this was the reconstruction model, not just a handful of NGO projects.

- Security expert — Shank can connect. Someone who thinks about energy as a military target.

## Part 2: Human Security — The American South

**Thesis:** The same vulnerability exists in the US. Centralized grids fail after storms. Communities that had distributed power kept their lights on. Asheville after Helene is the proof.

**Structure:**
- Open with Asheville. What happened when the grid went down. How long. What it meant for hospitals, schools, water systems — same categories as Ukraine.
- The communities or buildings that had solar + storage and what their experience was vs. everyone else.
- City council / local officials on what they learned and what they're pushing for now.
- Local activists or mutual aid organizers — who filled the gap when centralized systems failed.
- The parallel to Part 1: different threat (weather vs. missiles), identical vulnerability (centralized grid), identical solution (decentralize).
- Close with: this isn't a Ukraine problem or an Appalachia problem. It's an architecture problem.

**Interview sources:**
- Asheville city councilmember(s)
- Local energy resilience activists or mutual aid organizers
- Anyone running community solar or microgrid projects in western NC
- Possibly someone from a utility or grid operator willing to talk about the failure

## Part 3: Policy — The Case for Distributed Energy as National Security

**Thesis:** The US talks about energy security in terms of supply (oil, gas, imports). The real vulnerability is architecture. Centralized grids are single points of failure whether the threat is a foreign military, a hurricane, or a cyberattack. Distributed renewables are the fix.

**Structure:**
- Tie Parts 1 and 2 together. Same failure mode, different contexts.
- The current US policy framework treats renewables as climate policy. Reframe: this is defense infrastructure.
- What a national distributed energy resilience strategy would look like — not replacing the grid, supplementing it at critical facilities (hospitals, schools, water systems).
- The bipartisan case: energy independence is a security argument that works across the aisle.
- What Ukraine is learning right now that the US should be paying attention to.
- Close with the irony: Ukrainian communities under active bombardment are further ahead on energy resilience than American communities that just need to survive hurricane season.
